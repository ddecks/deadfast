Dune: Overhyped or Underappreciated?

tags
#Books #Scifi #Space #Frank-Herbert #Review
published
2024-11-28
reading time
2 minutes

Finally got around to reading Dune by Frank Herbert after Harrison kept recommending it. My honest take? It’s… complicated.

The Good

The first book is genuinely good. Herbert created this incredibly detailed universe with complex politics, ecology, and mysticism all woven together. The world-building is impressive, and you can see why it’s considered a sci-fi classic.

The movie (at least the first one) was visually stunning and did justice to the source material. Denis Villeneuve really understood the assignment.

The Not-So-Good

Here’s where I might get some hate: the rest of the series? Meh.

I know, I know - people love the entire saga, but after the first book, it felt like Herbert was trying too hard to expand on something that worked perfectly as a standalone story. The pacing gets weird, and some of the philosophical tangents feel forced.

Rating: 7.1/10

Yeah, that’s oddly specific, but that’s where it lands for me. It’s good, but maybe not the life-changing masterpiece everyone made it out to be.

Why It Still Matters

Despite my lukewarm feelings about the series as a whole, I get why Dune is important. It influenced basically every piece of space opera that came after it. The ecological themes, the political intrigue, the chosen one narrative - it’s all here first.

Plus, in our current climate crisis, Herbert’s focus on ecology and resource scarcity feels more relevant than ever.

Should You Read It?

If you’re into sci-fi at all, yes. Read the first book. It’s a classic for a reason, and you’ll understand so many references in other works once you do.

Just don’t feel obligated to read the entire series if the first one doesn’t completely blow you away. Sometimes one great book is enough.
